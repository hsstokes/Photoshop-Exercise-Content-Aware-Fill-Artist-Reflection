Photoshop Exercise — Content‑Aware Fill & Artist Reflection

Example outcome demonstrating use of Content‑Aware Fill and contextual artist research text.

Purpose: Combine Photoshop practical skills with artist research and reflection. Use Content‑Aware Fill to create experimental imagery from your project work, then add contextual notes with the Type Tool (T).

Recommended format: A3 landscape (420 × 297 mm) | 300 dpi | CMYK

Step 1 — Prepare Document

  • File → New → A3 Landscape
  • Resolution 300 dpi | Colour Mode CMYK
Create your A3 layout for consistent portfolio presentation.

Step 2 — Arrange Your Work

  • Import images of your design experiments.
  • You can do this by:
    • Dragging and dropping an image from your computer directly into the Photoshop window — it will appear as a new layer.
    • Or, from the menu, choose File → Place Embedded… to insert an image into your A3 document.
  • Use Move Tool (V) to position, and Cmd/Ctrl + T to resize or rotate each one.
  • Add a white background layer (Edit → Fill → White) so your work stands out clearly.
Balance the composition before adding effects.

Step 3 — Content‑Aware Fill (Expanding Ideas from Your Image)

  • Use the Rectangular Marquee Tool (M) to select a visually interesting area of your image — a texture, shape, or tone that stands out.
  • Go to Edit → Copy (Cmd/Ctrl +C) then Edit → Paste (Cmd/Ctrl + V). This creates a new layer holding just that fragment of your image.
  • In the Layers panel, switch off the 👁 (eye icon) for the original layer so you can focus on your new cut‑out.
  • Select the Magic Wand Tool (W) and click on the white areas around the shape to select the empty space.
  • Then go to Edit → Content‑Aware Fill… → adjust the sampling and preview → click OK. Photoshop will fill in the space, blending content from your image to form new unexpected textures and abstract patterns.
💡 Purpose: This approach encourages you to think and experiment abstractly — using your primary research to discover new forms, shapes, and compositions as part of idea development.

Step 4 — Refine and Blend

  • Merge layers and experiment with blending modes and opacity.
Blend layers for a unified effect.

Step 5 — Add Text & Artist Research

  • Use Type Tool (T) to write artist names, short quotes, and reflective notes.
  • Keep sentences short and clear — focus on meaning, not long text blocks.
Watch how to add and format text in Photoshop.

Step 5.1 — Typography & Text Layout

Aim: Make sure your text supports your images and feels professional and balanced.

  • Use clean, readable fonts – avoid overly decorative or “playful” typefaces such as Chalkduster.
  • Recommended: Andale Mono, Orator Std, Grandview Display (Body), or Typewriter Condensed.
  • Keep the text size modest: 9 – 14 pt is usually enough for A3 print layouts.
  • Use consistent spacing and alignment – left‑aligned or lightly centred text often works best.
  • Avoid placing text directly over busy image areas – give it space or use a semi‑transparent shape behind it.

Tip: Good text design shouldn’t compete with your images — it should help tell the story, quietly.

Step 6 — Finalise A3 Page

  • Ask yourself: Do my images connect and tell a story?
  • Does my layout help to show and enhance this story?
  • When finished, go to File → Save As → Photoshop (.PSD) — this keeps layers editable.
  • Then export as JPEG (.JPG) → ready for upload to Padlet or online review.

Artist Research & Reflection

Now choose your own artist — someone who connects to your idea or visual interest. The list below shows different approaches you can look at for inspiration, but you should find the one that best supports your story.

  • Gerhard Richter – Uses blurring to question memory and photography.
  • Lorna Simpson – Combines image and text to explore identity and language.
  • Andreas Gursky – Builds digital compositions from multiple images.
  • Ruth van Beek – Creates cut‑outs that suggest movement and emotion.
  • Sarah Charlesworth – Investigates how images communicate concepts.

Reflection prompts — add text directly on your A3 page:

  • Which artist did you choose, and why does their work connect to your images?
  • Include a short quote or idea from the artist that links with your theme (for example: memory, identity, place).
  • Write one or two sentences explaining what you learned from this artist — visually or conceptually.
  • Make sure to reference your sources correctly — this is marked and required for your portfolio submission. Include the artist’s name and book, website, or museum source. You can use the Harvard Referencing Guide & Generator to help you create accurate references.
